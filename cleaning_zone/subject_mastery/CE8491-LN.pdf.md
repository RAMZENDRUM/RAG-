# Specific Gravity

It is defined as the ratio of weight of a substance to the weight of an equal volume of water.

Specific gravity is the ratio of the weight of soil solids to the weight of an equal volume of water.

Formula: $q = \frac{q_{ra}}{q}$

Where:

- q = specific gravity of soil mass.

# Void Ratio

It is the ratio of volume of voids to total volume.

Formula: $e = \frac{V_v}{V}$

Where:

- Vv = volume of voids.
- V = total volume.

# Pore Ratio

It is the ratio of volume of voids to the volume of solids.

Formula: $n = \frac{V_v}{V_s}$

Where:

- Vs = volume of solids.

Put about 50g to 100g of wet soil in pycnometer and find mass (Ma)

Add water and stir it. Fill the pycnometer with water and find its mass (M3)

Empty the pycnometer, clean it and fill with clean water to hole of conical cap and fit it (M4)

# Standard Proctor Test

# Method Description

The test equipment consists of:

- Cylindrical metal mould, having an internal diameter of 10 in and a capacity of 0.30 cu ft.
- Detachable base plate
- Rammer 5.5 lb (2.5 kg) is dropped falling through a height of 12 inches (30.5 cm)

The test concludes in compacting soil at various water contents in the mould, in three equal layers, each layer being given 25 blows of the 5.5 lb rammer dropped from a height of 12 inches.

Dry density obtained on each test is determined by knowing the mass of compacted soil and its water content.

The compactive energy used for this test is 12,400 ft-lb per 1000 lb of soil.

# Compaction Test Procedure

The quantity of water to be added may be taken 4% for lower graded and 10% for finer graded soils.

The empty mould attached to the base plate is then filled with the soil. The mixed and natured soil is placed on the mould and compacted by giving a number of blows of a rammer uniformly distributed over the surface, such that the compacted height of soil is about 2/3 of the height of the mould.

The second and third layers are similarly compacted, each layer being 25 blows. The last compacted layer should project not more than 10 mm above the collar.

The collar is removed and excess soil is trimmed off to make it level with the top of the mould.

Weight of mould, base plate and compacted soil is taken. A representative sample is taken from the centre of the compacted specimen and kept for water content determination.

Bulk density (ρ) and dry density (Pd) for compacted soil are calculated from:

| ----- | ----- |
| V     | 1 + w |

M - mass of wet compacted specimen

V - Volume of mould.

The compacted soil is taken out to determine broken hand and remolded with water content (by 2 to 4%).

The test is repeated on soil samples with varying water content and density.

A compaction curve is plotted between water content as abscissa and dry density as ordinate.

# Falling Head Permeability Test

The constant head permeability test is conducted for coarse-grained soils only where a reasonable discharge can be collected in a short time. The falling head test is used for relatively less permeable soils where the discharge is small.

A standard pipe of known cross-sectional area is fed to a permeameter and water is allowed to run down. The water level in the standpipe constantly falls. Water flow operators are started after steady state has been reached. The head at any time t is equal to the difference in the water levels in the standpipe and the bottom of the bank.

Q is the total quantity of flow, and we have from Darcy's law:

q = Q = k i A

k = Q / (i A)

where:

- k = coefficient of permeability
- Q = total flow
- i = hydraulic gradient
- A = cross-sectional area

Thus, Q = k A i L

where L is the length of the flow path.

At any time t, the head h is equal to the difference in the water levels.

h2 = h1 - h0

t2 = t1 + Δt

h2 = h1 - (Q / (k A)) * t

where:

- h1 = initial head
- h0 = final head
- Δt = change in time

Note: The above equations are essential for understanding the flow through soils in the context of permeability testing.

# Pore Pressure

The pressure that builds up in pore water due to load increment on soil is termed as excess pore pressure or hydrostatic pressure or dynamic pressure, because it is in excess of the neutral pressure in water under saturated conditions. The excess hydrostatic pressure forces the water to drain out of voids. As water starts escaping from voids, the excess hydrostatic pressure in water gets gradually dissipated and the pressure is transferred as an effective pressure on solids and decreases in volume.

# 1. Consolidation of Soil

Consolidation is the process of pressure increment or consolidation on soil where no more water escapes from voids and a condition of equilibrium is altered.

# Hydrodynamic

Change of water in a saturated soil mass is called hydrodynamic log.

# Primary Consolidation

It is the reduction in volume of soil which is due primarily to squeezing out of water from pores. It is termed primary compression or consolidation or primary pore pressure effect.

# Secondary Consolidation

Even after the reduction of all excess hydrostatic pressure to zero, some compression of soil takes place at a very slow rate. This is known as secondary consolidation. During secondary compression, some water is expelled from the pores.

# Consolidation of Laterally Constrained Soil

If a remolded sample of laterally confined soil is placed in a consolidation apparatus, consisting of a metal ring and porous stones placed both at its top and bottom faces, the compression or consolidation of soil sample takes place under a vertical pressure applied on the top of porous stones. These porous stones provide free drainage of water and allow for the consolidation of the soil sample.

Under a given applied pressure, a final settlement and equilibrium condition is attained after certain time. At the equilibrium stage, the applied pressure naturally becomes effective pressure on soil. The pressure can then be increased and a new equilibrium condition is formed in the form of a curve.

# 1. What are the assumptions of Boussinesq Equations?

- The soil mass in homogeneous, that is all its constituent parts or elements are similar and it has identical properties at every point in it in identical directions.
- The soil mass is isotropic, that is it has identical elastic properties in all directions through any point of it.
- The soil mass is semi-infinite, that is it extends infinitely in all directions below a level surface.

# 2. Define isobar. (N/D'11) (A/M'11)

An isobar is a curved or contour connecting all points below the ground surface of equal vertical pressure.

# 3. Define pressure bulb or Stress isobar. (A/M'09, 16)

The zone in a loaded soil mass bounded by an isobar of given vertical pressure intensity is called a pressure bulb.

# 4. Write down the Boussinesq equations of vertical pressure due to concentrated load. (N/D'12, 16)

σ = 3Q / (2πz)

Q = point load

z = depth of stress acting

r = distance from the axis of load

# 5. Define Influence Value. (Nov 2013)

Newmark's chart consists of number of circles and sectors. Total elements for a nine circle chart will be 200. It is denoted by N. The value of 1/N (i.e., 1/200 or 0.005) is said to be the 'influence value' (or 'influence factor') for the chart. Each mesh may thus be understood to represent an influence area.

# 6. What is the principle behind the Newmark's influence chart? (A/M'17)

Newmark's Influence Chart is an illustration used to determine the vertical pressure at any point below a uniformly loaded flexible area of soil of any shape. This method, like others, was derived by integration of Boussinesq's equation for a point load.

# 7. Define consolidation and write its stages. (N/D’11) (A/M’16)

The process of gradual compression due to the expulsion of pore water under steady pressure is referred to as ‘Consolidation’. This is a time dependent phenomenon, especially occurs in clays.

- (i) Primary consolidation
- (ii) Secondary consolidation
- (iii) Tertiary compression

# 8. What are the factors which influence the compression behavior of soil? (N/D’15)

The compressibility of a soil depends on the:

- (i) Structural arrangement of the soil particles,
- (ii) Degree to which adjacent particles are bonded, in fine-grained soils.
- (iii) Pressure on the soil.

# 9. Write any four assumptions of Terzaghi’s theory of one dimensional consolidation. (A/M’10)

1. The soil is homogeneous (kz is independent of z).
2. The soil is completely saturated (S = 100%).
3. The soil grains and water are virtually incompressible (γw is constant and volume change of soil is only due to change in void ratio).
4. The compression is one-dimensional (u varies with z only).

# 10. Define isochrones. (A/M’09)

In a consolidation curve of a soil, the hydrostatic excess pressure will be maximum at the middle and it is zero at the top and bottom. The distribution of the hydrostatic excess pressure with depth is sinusoidal at other instants of time, as shown by dotted lines. These curves are called “Isochrones”.

# Planar Failure Surface

Planar failure surface encompasses a specific 30° departure on a plane of weakness on competent cohesive earth, consisting of 2 or 3 planar surfaces.

# Circular Failure Surface

An actual failure surface occurred. The rupture mass slides down a sliding surface in a definite pattern resembling a cycloid. Generally, the failure surfaces have somewhat flatter ends and sharper at the center. For simple idealized problems, the assumption of a circular failure surface is sufficiently accurate.

# Non-Circular Failure Surface

It occurs in many practical cases. The name is homogeneous due to one or more of the following:

1. Foundation of infinite depths.
2. Rigid boundary planes of material or zone shear.
3. Non-homogeneous earth dam.
4. Presence of soft layer in foundation.
